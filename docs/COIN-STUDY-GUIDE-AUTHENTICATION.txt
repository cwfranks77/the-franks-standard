SECTION 1 – HOW REAL COINS ARE MADE (AND WHY IT MATTERS)
==================================================

1.1 STRUCK VS CAST – THE CORE CONCEPT

Real coins from official mints are STRUCK:
- A blank planchet is placed between hardened steel dies.
- A press hits it with massive pressure.
- Metal flows into the design, creating sharp details and “flow lines”.

Most counterfeits are CAST:
- A real coin is used to make a mold.
- Molten metal is poured into the mold.
- The result is a copy with softer details and casting defects.

KEY DIFFERENCES:
- STRUCK: sharp edges, crisp letters, strong relief, cartwheel luster.
- CAST: rounded letters, mushy details, pits, bubbles, seam lines, dead luster.

If you remember nothing else: REAL = STRUCK, FAKE = USUALLY CAST.

--------------------------------------------------
1.2 METAL FLOW AND LUSTER
--------------------------------------------------

When a coin is struck, metal flows outward from the center toward the rim.
This creates microscopic lines called “flow lines”.

Under light, these flow lines create:
- “Cartwheel luster” on silver coins.
- A rotating, spinning light effect when you tilt the coin.

REAL SILVER COIN:
- Strong, directional luster.
- Light “rolls” around the surface.

FAKE / CAST COIN:
- Dull, flat, or “dead” surface.
- Light doesn’t move in a cartwheel pattern.
- Often looks grainy or powdery under magnification.

--------------------------------------------------
1.3 WHY CERTAIN COINS ARE COUNTERFEITED MORE
--------------------------------------------------

Counterfeiters go where the money is.

HIGH-VALUE TARGETS:
- Morgan Dollars: 1885-CC, 1889-CC, 1893-S, key dates and CC mint marks.
- Early U.S. gold.
- Key-date cents and nickels.
- Modern bullion: American Silver Eagles, Maple Leafs, Krugerrands.

RULE:
If a coin is worth a lot more than its metal value, it WILL be faked.

Your job is to know:
- Which dates are dangerous.
- What genuine examples look like.
- How fakes usually fail.

SECTION 2 – UNIVERSAL AUTHENTICATION FRAMEWORK
==================================================

This framework works for ANY coin, any country, any era.

2.1 THE 10-POINT CHECKLIST

1) WEIGHT
- Every coin has a specific official weight.
- Use a digital scale with at least 0.01g resolution.
- If weight is off by more than about 0.2g (for most classic coins), that’s a red flag.

2) DIAMETER
- Use calipers.
- Compare to official specs.
- Even 0.1mm off can be suspicious.

3) THICKNESS
- Counterfeiters often get thickness wrong.
- Too thick or too thin = red flag.

4) METAL / MAGNET TEST
- Silver, gold, copper, and most legit alloys are NOT magnetic.
- If a coin that should be non-magnetic sticks to a magnet → FAKE.

5) EDGE / REEDING
- Check the edge:
  - Reeded? Plain? Lettered?
- On reeded edges:
  - Real: sharp, even, consistent.
  - Fake: soft, uneven, shallow, or irregular.

6) LETTER SHARPNESS
- Look at letters under magnification.
- Real: crisp serifs, sharp corners, clean shapes.
- Fake: rounded, swollen, “melted” look, filled-in holes.

7) DESIGN DETAILS
- Compare to a known genuine example.
- Look at:
  - Eyes
  - Hair
  - Feathers
  - Stars
  - Date
- Fakes often look “off” in the fine details.

8) SURFACE TEXTURE
- Real: metallic, crystalline, with natural wear patterns.
- Fake: grainy, bubbly, pitted, or “cast” texture.

9) SOUND TEST (“PING TEST”)
- Tap the coin gently with another coin or a non-damaging object.
- Real silver: clear, ringing tone that lasts.
- Fake: dull, short, or “clunky” sound.

10) CONTEXT AND PRICE
- If the price is way below market for a key date or rare mint mark, assume it’s fake until proven otherwise.

--------------------------------------------------
2.2 TRICKS OF THE TRADE – QUICK VISUAL TESTS
--------------------------------------------------

TILT TEST:
- Tilt the coin slowly under a single light source.
- Look for cartwheel luster on silver coins.
- No cartwheel = suspicious, especially on “uncirculated” coins.

RIM SHADOW TEST:
- Look at the rim under angled light.
- Real: sharp, defined rim with a clean shadow.
- Fake: soft, fuzzy, or uneven rim.

DENTICLE RHYTHM:
- Denticles are the “teeth” around the rim.
- Real: evenly spaced, consistent size.
- Fake: irregular spacing, some shallow, some deep, some missing.

EYE TEST:
- On portrait coins, the eye is hard to fake.
- Real: defined eyelid, pupil, and shape.
- Fake: blob, smear, or soft, cartoonish eye.

METAL FLOW TEST:
- Look for radial lines from center to rim (especially on silver).
- Real: visible flow lines under magnification.
- Fake: no flow lines, or random grain.

SECTION 3 – MORGAN DOLLAR SPECIALIST SECTION
==================================================

3.1 BASIC MORGAN DOLLAR FACTS

- Minted: 1878–1904, 1921.
- Composition: 90% silver, 10% copper.
- Weight: 26.73 grams.
- Diameter: 38.1 mm.
- Edge: Reeded.

KEY MINTS:
- No mint mark: Philadelphia.
- CC: Carson City (highly desirable, heavily counterfeited).
- O: New Orleans.
- S: San Francisco.
- D: Denver (1921 only).

3.2 WHY 1885-CC, 1889-CC, 1893-S ARE DANGEROUS

These dates are worth serious money even in low grades.
That makes them prime targets for counterfeiters.

If you see:
- 1885-CC
- 1889-CC
- 1893-S

…your suspicion level should automatically go up.

3.3 LIBERTY DIAGNOSTICS (OBVERSE)

Focus on the word LIBERTY on the headband.

REAL:
- L: sharp vertical and horizontal strokes.
- I: straight, clean.
- B: clear loops, not merged.
- E: sharp top bar, clear middle bar, open shape.
- R: defined leg and serif.
- T, Y: crisp, angular.

FAKE:
- E looks like an O or C (mushy, filled-in).
- R is rounded or smeared.
- Letters look swollen or “melted” into the band.
- Serifs are missing or soft.

3.4 CC MINT MARK DIAGNOSTICS (REVERSE)

Location:
- Above the DO in DOLLAR.
- Between the wreath and the bow.

REAL CC:
- Small, crisp letters.
- Even spacing between the two C’s.
- Correct height above the bow.
- Sharp serifs and clean edges.

FAKE CC:
- Too big or too small.
- Too low, touching the bow or wreath.
- Uneven spacing between C’s.
- Rounded, mushy, or “bubbly” edges.
- Looks like it was added later or tooled.

3.5 COMMON FAKE MORGAN PATTERNS

- Cast copies with:
  - Soft stars.
  - Mushy hair.
  - Weak eagle feathers.
  - Rounded denticles.

- Added mint marks:
  - Real coin from a common date/mint.
  - Mint mark added or altered to look like CC or S.
  - Look for tooling marks, scratches, or disturbed metal around mint mark.

SECTION 4 – FAKE VS REAL COMPARISON (TEXT-ONLY DESCRIPTIONS)
==================================================

4.1 REAL MORGAN VS FAKE MORGAN – OBVERSE

REAL:
- Liberty’s hair: individual strands visible, especially above forehead and behind ear.
- Eye: sharp eyelid, defined pupil.
- LIBERTY: crisp letters, no merging, no blobs.
- Stars: sharp points, evenly spaced.

FAKE:
- Hair: clumps, no fine strands, looks “melted”.
- Eye: blob or smear, no sharp eyelid.
- LIBERTY: E and R often rounded or filled.
- Stars: rounded points, some uneven or misshapen.

4.2 REAL MORGAN VS FAKE MORGAN – REVERSE

REAL:
- Eagle’s breast feathers: layered, defined.
- Wing feathers: clear separation, sharp edges.
- Wreath leaves: veins visible, sharp edges.
- UNITED STATES OF AMERICA: crisp letters, no swelling.

FAKE:
- Eagle’s breast: flat, featureless, or lumpy.
- Wings: feathers blend together.
- Wreath: leaves look like blobs.
- Letters: thick, swollen, or uneven.

4.3 REAL VS FAKE – EDGE

REAL:
- Reeded edge: consistent, sharp, evenly spaced.
- No seam line.

FAKE:
- Reeded edge: shallow, uneven, or partially missing.
- Sometimes a faint seam line around the edge (casting seam).

SECTION 5 – GENERAL COIN TYPES AND WHAT TO WATCH FOR
==================================================

5.1 SILVER BULLION (E.G., AMERICAN SILVER EAGLES)

REAL ASE:
- Weight: 31.103 g (1 troy ounce).
- Diameter: 40.6 mm.
- Edge: Reeded.
- Strong, bright luster and sharp details.

FAKE ASE:
- Wrong weight (often 28–32 g).
- Wrong diameter or thickness.
- Dead or strange luster.
- Off-looking fonts or design details.

5.2 COPPER COINS (E.G., INDIAN HEAD, LINCOLN CENTS)

REAL:
- Sharp lettering.
- Clear date and mint mark.
- Natural brown or red-brown toning.

FAKE:
- Blurry letters.
- Wrong style of date or mint mark.
- Artificial color or strange surface.

5.3 NICKELS (BUFFALO, JEFFERSON)

REAL:
- Strong design details in hair, bison fur, etc.
- Correct fonts and spacing.

FAKE:
- Soft details.
- Wrong shape of numbers or letters.
- Surfaces look “off” under magnification.

SECTION 6 – BUILDING YOUR AUTHENTICATOR MINDSET
==================================================

6.1 SUSPICION FIRST, TRUST LATER

For high-value coins:
- Assume fake until proven real.
- Let the coin EARN your trust.

6.2 COMPARE, COMPARE, COMPARE

Always compare:
- To a known genuine example.
- Same date, same mint mark, same type.

Over time, your brain learns:
- The “look” of genuine coins.
- The “feel” of fakes.

6.3 DOCUMENT EVERYTHING

When you examine a coin:
- Write down:
  - Weight
  - Diameter
  - Thickness
  - Magnet result
  - Visual notes
- This becomes your personal reference.

6.4 CONTINUOUS LEARNING

To keep leveling up:
- Study certified coins (PCGS, NGC, ANACS).
- Look at auction archives.
- Read grading and authentication books.
- Follow counterfeit alerts from major grading services.

==================================================
SECTION 7 – CERTIFICATION ROADMAP (HIGH LEVEL)
==================================================

7.1 LEARN THE BASICS (YOU’RE ALREADY HERE)

- Understand how coins are made.
- Understand how they are faked.
- Practice with common types (Morgans, ASEs, etc.).

7.2 STUDY GRADING

- Learn the Sheldon scale (AG to MS).
- Learn how wear progresses.
- Learn to separate wear from weak strike.

7.3 STUDY COUNTERFEITS

- Look at known fake examples.
- Learn common fake patterns.
- Focus on key dates and key types.

7.4 FORMAL EDUCATION (IF YOU CHOOSE)

- American Numismatic Association (ANA) courses.
- Grading/authentication seminars.
- Books by major grading services.

7.5 BUILD A PORTFOLIO

- Keep records of coins you’ve authenticated.
- Keep photos and notes.
- This becomes your “resume” as an authenticator.
